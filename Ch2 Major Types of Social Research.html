Chapter 2: Types of Social Research

A Roadmap to the Dimensions of Inquiry

A Universe of Research

Social research isn't one-size-fits-all. Every study can be categorized along several key dimensions. Understanding these dimensions helps you identify and classify any research you encounter.

Dimension 1: Use & Audience

Who is the research for, and how will it be used? This is the most fundamental distinction.

üéì

Basic Research

Also called academic or pure research, its primary audience is the scientific community. The main goal is to advance fundamental knowledge and test theories. It's driven by curiosity and the desire to understand how the social world works.

Key Question: "Is this theoretically interesting?"

üí°

Applied Research

This research is for practitioners, policymakers, and the public. The goal is to solve specific, real-world problems. The findings are meant to be used for decision-making. While rigorous, it's less focused on theory and more on practical application.

Key Question: "Does this help solve a problem?"

Three Major Types of Applied Research:

Evaluation Research

+

The most widely used type. It asks: "Does this program or policy work?"
Formative Evaluation: Done during a program to make improvements.
Summative Evaluation: Done after a program to assess its overall effectiveness.

Action Research

+

Treats knowledge as a form of power and aims to empower those being studied. It's an iterative process where the researcher and participants work together to diagnose a problem, take action, and evaluate the results. Key themes are participation and empowerment.

Social Impact Assessment (SIA)

+

Estimates the likely social consequences of a planned change (e.g., a new highway, a new factory). It helps communities and planners anticipate and manage social effects before they happen.

Dimension 3: The Time Dimension

Is the research a snapshot in time or a movie?

üì∏

Cross-Sectional

The most common type. It observes a collection of people at one single point in time. It's simple and cost-effective, but cannot capture social change or processes.

üéûÔ∏è

Longitudinal

Observes people at multiple points in time. It's more complex and expensive, but extremely powerful for studying change. There are three main types:

Time-Series Study

+

Collects the same type of information from a group or population across multiple time periods, but from different people. Example: Surveying 1,000 U.S. citizens about unemployment every year.

Panel Study

+

Observes the exact same people, group, or organization across multiple time periods. The most powerful type, but very difficult and expensive. Example: Surveying the same 1,000 citizens every year for a decade.

Cohort Study

+

Focuses on a category of people who share a similar life experience in a specified period (e.g., all people born in 1990, all couples married in 2020). It tracks these people over time.

Dimension 4: Data Collection

How is the data actually gathered?

üìä

Quantitative

Data is in the form of numbers. This approach measures objective facts, focuses on variables, and uses statistics for analysis. Common techniques include:

  • Experiments
  • Surveys
  • Content Analysis (counting)
  • Existing Statistics
üí¨

Qualitative

Data is in the form of words, pictures, or objects. This approach focuses on interpreting cultural meaning, understanding social reality, and paying attention to context. Common techniques include:

  • Interviews
  • Field Research (Ethnography)
  • Historical Research
