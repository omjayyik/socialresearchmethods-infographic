How We *Usually* Acquire Knowledge

Knowledge from Authority

We trust experts like parents, teachers, and doctors. This is a quick and easy way to learn. However, authorities can be wrong, they may disagree, and they might speak on fields outside their expertise. A true expert requires a community of peers to recognize them. Relying solely on authority means we stop thinking for ourselves.

Knowledge from Tradition

Tradition is "the way things have always been." It's authority from the past. We accept it without question (e.g., "An apple a day keeps the doctor away"). While some traditions hold wisdom, others can become outdated or carry historical prejudices forward without scrutiny.

Knowledge from Common Sense

We rely on what "everyone knows." Common sense is valuable for daily life, but it can be contradictory ("opposites attract" vs. "birds of a feather flock together") and often contains logical fallacies. What's common sense in one culture can be nonsense in another.

Knowledge from Media Myths

TV shows, movies, and news often prioritize entertainment over accuracy. This can create powerful, distorted images of reality‚Äîlike portraying most criminals as evil masterminds, when in reality, crime is more complex. These myths can shape our understanding in misleading ways.

Knowledge from Personal Experience

"Seeing is believing" is a powerful, but flawed, principle. What we personally experience is true for us, but it's often not generalizable. This reliance leads to several errors in reasoning...

The 5 Big Errors of Human Inquiry

Relying on personal experience leads to these common mental traps. Social research is specifically designed to avoid them.
Click each error to learn more.

1. Overgeneralization

+

The Error: Believing that a few similar events are evidence of a general pattern.
Example: You have a few bad experiences with a specific brand of laptop and conclude that the entire brand is unreliable.
Scientific Guard: Using large, representative samples of subjects or events to ensure findings are not based on a few fluke cases.

2. Selective Observation

+

The Error: Once you believe a pattern exists, you start focusing only on future events that confirm it, ignoring those that don't.
Example: If you believe people from a certain political party are angry, you'll notice every angry person from that party and ignore all the calm ones, and also ignore angry people from other parties.
Scientific Guard: Specifying in advance the number and kind of observations to be made and having colleagues look for "deviant cases."

3. Premature Closure

+

The Error: Feeling you have the answer and no longer needing to listen, seek information, or ask questions.
Example: After doing a quick internet search on a complex topic like climate change, you decide you know everything and refuse to consider new evidence.
Scientific Guard: The entire scientific enterprise, which is a continuous process of exploration and is open to questioning everything.

4. The Halo Effect

+

The Error: Overgeneralizing from what you believe to be a highly positive or prestigious source.
Example: Accepting a statement as true simply because it was published by a prestigious university or a famous person, without critically evaluating the evidence yourself.
Scientific Guard: Peer review and replication. Research is judged on its own merits, not on the reputation of the author.

5. False Consensus

+

The Error: A psychological tendency to assume our own beliefs, values, and experiences are shared by the majority.
Example: "I support this policy, so I'm sure most reasonable people do too."
Scientific Guard: Conscious, systematic sampling and data collection to see what others actually think and do, rather than assuming.

The Scientific Approach & Community

Science is more than a set of techniques; it's a social institution with a unique culture. Researchers follow a set of professional norms that guide their work.

Universalism

Research is judged only on the basis of its scientific merit, regardless of who or where the researcher is.

Organized Skepticism

Scientists must challenge and question all evidence and subject every study to intense scrutiny.

Disinterestedness

Scientists must be neutral, impartial, and open to unexpected findings, even if they go against their own beliefs.

Communalism

Scientific knowledge is public knowledge. New findings are shared freely and belong to everyone.

Honesty

Academic misconduct like plagiarism or faking data is a major taboo and a career-ending offense.

The Goal

To produce a body of knowledge that is constantly growing, changing, and being refined through rigorous testing.
